Section 1 - Acquire

This stage involves searching for datasets that I could later use in Tableau to create visualisations. It took me a while to find a suitable datatset. I eventually settled on a crime dataset that detailed crime (violent and non-violent) in the US from 1960 - 2019 that was on a GitHub and sourced from the FBI. I got two more datasets one also sourced from the FBI and the last from Kaggle. These datasets dealt with various factors that may contribute to crimes such as poverty rate and median household income from the second dataset and opioid deaths from the third dataset.They were all formatted the same way and contained a key column so they could be connected in Tableau later.

Section 4 - Mine

The Mine Stage involves looking for any meaningful data or patterns before visualisations begin with the remaining data. At this stage I created a calculated field by adding two columns togehter (violent and non-violent crime per state) and took the years and divided them up into decades and called this new column "Decades". I was also able to connect the datasets through their key column "State" so I could compare all three. Finally, I conducted a R-Squared statistical test to find how related population was to both violent and non-violent crime. It was slightly related to non-violent but not so much for violent.

Conclusion

My conclusions from the CA.

Democrat states were elected less but yet they had higher overall crime than Republican states. This could be due to some states being more populated than the others but this was measured per capita (per 100K). Split states were the most common and had to most crime. Crime was also at it's worst in the 1980's and 1990's. This could be due to less reporting or proof due to the lack of technology back then (e.g., smartphones).

Population also wasn’t a leading factor for crime being committed either. It was also hard to pinpoint an exact cause for causes for crimes as the different potential factors didn’t point to any one conclusion (e.g., Maryland was in the top three for crime overall but had relatively low unemployment, opioid deaths and poverty and a decently high median household income). The likelihood is that these factors do contribute to some degree but the problems for crime may stem deeper.

Overall, there were some interesting discoveries that were made but I would need more data and time to draw more definitive conclusions about why crime might be the way that it is and what in recent decades had mitigated it compared to the earlier decades.
